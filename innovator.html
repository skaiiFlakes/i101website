  • 1967

    Breazal's Early Years

    Cynthia Breazeal was born in 1967 in New Mexico as a daughter of two scientists. She later moved with her family to grow up in California.  Growing up she had a piqued interest in robots after watching Star Wars. She had guidance from her parents to pursue her dreams of creating robots that could integrate with humans and be used for social good.

    She got her Bachelor of Science in Electrical and Computer Engineering from the University of California in Santa Barbara in 1989. After that, she was still ready to take on more. In 1993, Breazeal applied to the Massachusetts Institute of Technology and was accepted to complete a master's in electrical engineering. Breazeal also went on to complete her PhD at MIT as well in electrical engineering and computing in 2000 (Wikipedia).

  • 1998

    Kismet

    While Breazeal was at MIT earning her Master’s, she was apart of a team who created Kismet, a robot head that was programmed to sense human emotions based off of ques. The robot vitalized human psychology to make it resemble human emotions as closely as possible. Kismet has movable eyelids, eyebrows, mouth, and ears to give a wide range of emotions. The robot can “express” moods such as anger, fear, sadness or sleep. However, it is important to note, even from Breazeal herself, Kismet is not conscious as she states in her book. Breazeal reported it took 2.5 years of full-time work to get the code behind Kismet to work.

    Kismet has motion sensors that can detect eye contact and even skin color. Breazeal describes her relationship such as a caretaker teaching an infant about the world. In a video titled, “How Kismet Works” on YouTube, the creators behind the robot explain how he works. Kismet wants social stimulation and becomes “sad” when he doesn’t sense anyone or have any stimuli. Kismet’s voice is similar to infant babbling but changes tone to convey an emotion more clearly how a human would.

  • 2002

    Designing Sociable Robots

    In 2002, Breazeal published her book diving into the intricacies and possibilities of social robotics. Breazeal strongly advocates for robots to integrate with human life to not only serve us, but to communicate and serve as companions. The publication has a strong tie to her work done at MIT with Kismet, the robot head. She discusses what went into developing the complex robot, such as studying animal and human behavior as well as learning about animatronics.

  • 2002

    Leonardo

    Named after the amazing artist, Leonardo is a robotic piece of art. Breazeal finished working on this robot in 2002. He is similar to Kismet, but was given a warmer appearance. Instead of the metal wiring needed for Kismet showing, Leonardo is wrapped in fur and less stereotypical robot-like.

    Aside from appearance, Leonardo is more complicated than Kismet. He has over 50 motors to control movement to appear as lifelike as possible. He has abilites such as looking where humans tell him to, mimicing people he is around and he can even explore on his own. The goal with Leonardo was to have an untrianed person be able to interact with him and see the benefits of social robotics.

  • 2011

    Kombusto

    This dragon-like robot, named Kombusto, was designed to act as a teaching aid for pre-schoolers. It’s made from an android phone that users see as his face, which may seem less intricate than previous designs, but it does keep the cost low. Keeping down the cost would allow this robot to possible be used in a variety of schools, not only affluent ones.

    Pico is a DragonBot who was created to test if robots could aid teaching and increase curiosity in children. MIT sampled their design with real children who used the robot to tell and create stories by their input. The children could click and drag images and allow their creativity to take them where they liked. In the study, they found all the children over the two month period expanded their vocabulary, created new stories and overall, enjoyed the social robot. Pico outputs were done by humans, but better technology advancement could be used.

  • 2017

    Jibo

    Jibo is Breazeal’s most recent robot meant for the home and everyday people. This jumps off of Leonardo, as the goal was for untrained people to be able to interact with him. Jibo’s key feature is household integreation. Jibo could take family photos so everyone is included, remind users of their schedule and track faces. Jibo is able to connect with all ages, such as by storytelling for children.

    Breazeal’s design is a sharp contrast from her past work. Jibo has a sleek, monochrome design instead of fur and more child friendly. Breazeal speaks on this by stating the design choice was deliberate, and she aims for Jibo to be seen as a useful tool and not a toy.

  • 2024

    Present

    Breazeal now currently teaches at MIT and leads the Personal Robots Group in the Media Lab. Using her expertise in this field, she leads MIT students with new research while providing expert insight. She is the director of MIT RAISE, which aims to use AI in schools from kindergarten through high school and beyond.

    As of 2024, she creates videos on YouTube as she believes in education for all. Her topics typically include innovations in artificial intelligence and how society can integrate AI in daily life. She recently was awarded the 2024 Robotics Medal from MassRobotics.
